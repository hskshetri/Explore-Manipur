About Manipur

Manipur, also known by its ancient and revered name Kangleipak, a jeweled land nestled in the lush, verdant corner of Northeast India. Located roughly between 23°83’N to 25°68’N latitude and 93°03’E to 94°78’E longitude, this enchanting state is often hailed as the 'Switzerland of the East' for its stunning landscapes.

Manipur is surrounded by several prominent hill ranges, including the Naga Hills, Mizo Hills, Chin Hills, East Manipur Hills, and West Manipur Hills. These hills form a natural boundary around the fertile Imphal Valley and contribute richly to the state’s diverse ecology and vibrant cultural mosaic.

Geographically, Manipur shares its borders with the Indian states of Nagaland to the north, Assam to the west, and Mizoram to the south, while its eastern boundary meets the international border with Myanmar (Burma). This strategic location makes Manipur a vital gateway between India and Southeast Asia.

The state is composed of 16 districts, each with its unique charm and heritage: Imphal East, Imphal West, Thoubal, Bishnupur, Churachandpur, Chandel, Senapati, Ukhrul, Tamenglong, Kangpokpi, Kamjong, Tengnoupal, Pherzawl, Jiribam, Kakching, and Noney.

At the heart of Manipur lies Loktak Lake, the largest freshwater lake in Northeast India, famed for its unique floating islands called Phumdis. These phumdis provide a sanctuary for the rare Sangai deer, the state animal, making the lake a biodiversity hotspot. Nearby, Keibul Lamjao National Park holds the distinction of being the world’s only floating national park, preserving this fragile ecosystem and the endangered Sangai.

Manipur’s blend of ancient traditions, natural wonders, and vibrant communities offers a unique window into the spirit of Northeast India. From the legendary sport of Polo, said to have originated here, to the rhythmic dances and colorful festivals, this land continues to captivate all who visit.
